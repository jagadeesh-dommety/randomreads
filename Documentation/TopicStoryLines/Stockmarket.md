The Berkshire "Mistake": Warren Buffett bought Berkshire Hathaway (a failing textile mill) out of spite because the owner tried to cheat him out of 12 cents per share. He calls it his "biggest mistake" despite its $900B+ value today.
Nintendo’s Love Hotel Roots: Before stocks and video games, Nintendo was a publicly traded company that ran "love hotels" and a taxi service.



Bernie Madoff’s "Boring" Fraud: Unlike most scammers who promise 100% returns, Madoff promised a steady, boring 10%. It worked because it looked "too realistic" to be a scam.
Wirecard’s Missing $2 Billion: In 2020, this German tech darling admitted that $2 billion on its balance sheet simply didn't exist. It was a massive failure of auditors who never checked the bank accounts.
The Salad Oil Swindle (1963): A trader filled tanks with water and put a thin layer of salad oil on top to fool inspectors into thinking he had millions in collateral.
The "Flash Crash" Kid: In 2010, a lone trader in his parents' basement in London used an algorithm to "spoof" the market, contributing to a $1 trillion market crash in 36 minutes.



Bogle’s "Folly": When Jack Bogle launched the first Index Fund in 1975, it was called "un-American" because it didn't try to "beat" the market. Today, it controls trillions.
The Monkey vs. The Pro: In multiple famous studies, a blindfolded monkey throwing darts at a stock list outperformed the majority of high-paid Wall Street fund managers.

The $100 Million Deli: In 2021, a single deli in New Jersey was the only asset of a publicly traded company valued at $100 million, highlighting the absurdity of "shell company" trading.

The Shoesblack Indicator: In 1929, Joe Kennedy (father of JFK) sold all his stocks because his shoeshine boy gave him stock tips. He realized that when the "last person" enters the market, the bubble is about to burst.



Loss Aversion: Psychologically, the pain of losing $1,000 on a stock is twice as powerful as the joy of gaining $1,000. This causes people to "hold losers" until they hit zero.
The Hindenburg Omen: A technical indicator named after the disaster, used by traders to predict an imminent market crash based on the number of stocks hitting new highs vs. new lows.

The Sentiment Machine: Modern hedge funds use AI to scan "X" (Twitter) and Reddit for keywords to trade stocks before humans even read the news.
The Inverse Cramer: A popular "meme" strategy where investors trade the exact opposite of what TV personality Jim Cramer recommends, which eventually became a real-life tracked index.
